# Detecting the critical bit-width for superposition collapse

A pre-registered experimental protocol can determine whether **a sharp phase transition in SAE-recoverable feature structure occurs between INT4 and INT2 quantization**, with model width acting as the finite-size scaling parameter. The hypothesis is testable on a single 4×A100 80GB node using Gemma-2 2B/9B with Gemma Scope JumpReLU SAEs as the primary asset, HQQ as a uniform 1–8 bit quantizer that preserves FP16 activations (essential for SAE hooks), and Pythia 410M→6.9B as a controlled scaling ladder. The closest published precedent — Liu et al.'s 2026 "From Signal Degradation to Computation Collapse" (arXiv:2604.19884) — already describes a qualitative cliff at the 4→2 bit transition, but no work has extracted critical exponents, performed finite-size scaling, or tested Michaud-style frequency ordering of feature loss. **That is the gap this experiment fills.** The design separates three confounders that any sharp-looking transition could secretly be: (i) quantization-method artifact, (ii) trivial activation distribution drift, and (iii) the metric-induced "mirage" critique of Schaeffer et al. Below, I lay out the full protocol.

## What the hypothesis predicts and what would refute it

The hypothesis makes three coupled claims. **First**, there exists a threshold b\* in bits below which features SAEs find in the FP16 model become linearly unrecoverable from quantized activations. **Second**, the transition is *sharp* — well-modelled by a step or high-Hill-coefficient sigmoid rather than a smooth power-law decay. **Third**, the transition is intrinsic to information capacity, not method-specific noise, so it persists across quantization algorithms and survives the matched-MSE Gaussian-noise control.

A clean confirmation looks like this: a Hill-fit coefficient n with bootstrap 95% CI excluding n<3; a step model with ΔBIC > 10 over a smooth sigmoid; a susceptibility (cross-seed variance of the order parameter) that peaks at b\* and grows with model width d; data-collapse of curves at different d onto a single master function under finite-size rescaling; and quantitative agreement of b\* (within ±0.5 bits) across HQQ, GPTQ, and AQLM. A clean refutation looks like: monotonic smooth degradation with no susceptibility peak, no collapse, b\* drifting freely with method or calibration set, and full restoration of metrics by a simple per-channel affine correction of mean/variance shift.

## Models, SAEs, and the SAE-training decision

**Primary system: Gemma-2 2B with Gemma Scope canonical residual-stream JumpReLU SAEs** at layers {0, 6, 12, 19, 25}, width 16k, with width-65k SAEs at layer 12 as a width-robustness check. Gemma Scope is the densest open SAE asset in existence — every layer × three sites × multiple widths × 5–6 L0 targets, CC-BY-4.0, JumpReLU architecture, integrated into SAELens via one-line `SAE.from_pretrained()` calls. **Secondary system: Gemma-2 9B**, same SAE family at layers {0, 10, 20, 30, 41}, for a 4× scaling step within identical architecture. **Tertiary system: Pythia 410M / 1.4B / 2.8B / 6.9B** with EleutherAI/sparsify TopK SAEs (32× expansion, every residual layer) provides a five-point scaling ladder under simpler architecture — essential for the finite-size scaling analysis.

The choice of SAE-training strategy is the most consequential interpretability decision. The literature (Gupte et al. arXiv:2507.15977; Borobia et al. arXiv:2603.25325) shows that **FP16-trained SAEs transfer to moderately compressed models with only mild degradation**, but the regime of interest here is precisely where transfer is expected to fail. The protocol therefore uses **both**: the FP16 reference SAE applied to every bit-width is one measurement axis (probes whether the *same* features still live in the quantized activations); a fresh JumpReLU SAE trained per bit-width in the critical region b∈{2, 3, 4} is the other (probes whether *any* superposition structure remains, just in a different basis). The gap between these two — features that disappear from the FP16-SAE evaluation but reappear in the quantized-SAE — is itself a diagnostic of geometric reorganization without information destruction.

Because Paulo & Belrose (arXiv:2501.16615) show that SAEs trained on identical data with different seeds share only ~30% of features at cos≥0.7, **every condition must be replicated with at least three SAE seeds** to obtain non-trivial error bars on cross-condition feature matching.

## Quantization pipeline and the continuous-bit-width problem

**HQQ is the primary quantizer.** It uniquely satisfies all four requirements: it supports the full 1–8 bit range with a single algorithm (no method confounding across the sweep), requires no calibration data (no calibration-set confounding), has native HuggingFace Transformers integration via `HqqConfig`, and crucially **preserves FP16 activations** at inference because HQQLinear dequantizes weights into the FP16 matmul on the fly. This means standard `register_forward_hook` calls on residual-stream sites return FP16 tensors identical in dtype, shape, and basis to the FP16 baseline — the only thing the SAE sees changing across bit-widths is the *content* of the activations. Group size is fixed at 64 throughout.

**Robustness rungs** at the critical bit-widths use GPTQ (via `llm-compressor` GPTQModifier, W4A16/W3A16/W2A16, 128×2048-token RedPajama-v2 calibration) and **AQLM-1×16 pre-quantized 2-bit checkpoints** for the published-SOTA 2-bit reference point. Activation-quantizing methods (SmoothQuant W8A8, QuaRot, SpinQuant, BitNet b1.58) are **excluded** because they return integer or rotation-shifted activations that break SAE-hook compatibility.

The fundamental obstacle to detecting sharpness is that bit-width is discrete, providing only ~5 grid points between FP16 and INT2. Four complementary interpolation strategies generate near-continuous effective bpw. **Group-size sweeps at fixed nominal bits** (g ∈ {256, 128, 64, 32, 16, 8} at each of k=2,3,4) vary effective bpw smoothly because per-group scale and zero-point overhead is fixed-cost. **Mixed-precision allocation** via HQQ's `dynamic_config` assigns fraction f of layers (chosen randomly, by depth, or by Hessian sensitivity à la HAWQ-V2) to k+1 bits and (1−f) to k bits, sweeping f∈[0,1]. **Calibrated additive Gaussian noise** on FP16 weights with variance Δ²/12 = R²/(12·4^b) reproduces the MSE of integer quantization with a fully continuous knob — the gap between this curve and real-quantization curves at matched MSE is a direct measure of how much *structure* of quantization noise (versus its energy) matters. **Codebook-size sweeps** using residual VQ (Kamath's RQAE approach) span k∈{4, 16, 64, …, 65536} as a continuous proxy. Cross-method agreement on the location of b\* in effective-bpw units is the principal evidence that any observed transition is intrinsic rather than method-specific.

## Order parameters and measurement protocol

The experimental design uses a panel of **three primary order parameters** chosen for sensitivity, boundedness, and theoretical interpretability, plus a wide set of secondary diagnostics.

**P1 — Shared-feature fraction (Hungarian-matched, cos ≥ 0.7 bidirectional).** This is Paulo & Belrose's (2025) metric. Decoder columns of the FP16 reference SAE and the bit-width-b SAE are matched via the Jonker–Volgenant assignment maximizing total cosine; features count as "shared" only when both encoder and decoder vectors clear the 0.7 threshold. The metric is bounded in [0,1], mechanistically interpretable as "fraction of features that survived as recognizable directions," and immune to MMCS's many-to-one inflation problem.

**P2 — Loss-recovered and KL divergence to FP16.** Cross-entropy recovered when patching SAE reconstructions into quantized forward passes (Bricken-style: LR = 1 − (CE_SAE − CE_clean)/(CE_zero − CE_clean)) directly measures *functional* preservation, and pairs naturally with the next-token-logit KL divergence between FP16 and quantized models. KL is the natural thermodynamic free-energy analog and dominates in the analysis when comparing transition sharpness across model sizes.

**P3 — Debiased linear CKA between matched-layer activations**, using the Murphy et al. 2024 unbiased estimator on N≫d tokens to avoid Kornblith-bias inflation. The Davari critiques are addressed by reporting Procrustes distance (a true metric satisfying the triangle inequality, per Williams et al. 2021) as a parallel geometric measure.

The **composite phase indicator** Φ(b) = (LR(b) + SharedFrac(b) + CKA_debiased(b))/3, all normalized to FP16=1, is fit to a Hill function Φ(b) = (b/b\*)ⁿ / (1+(b/b\*)ⁿ) by nonlinear least squares; bootstrap over SAE seeds and calibration resamples gives 95% confidence intervals on b\* and on the **Hill coefficient n, which directly quantifies sharpness**. A second composite is fit to a step function and a piecewise-linear model; BIC differences identify the best-supported regime.

Secondary diagnostics report mechanism rather than transition location: L0 sparsity drift, fraction of variance unexplained, dead-feature counts, log-density histograms of feature firing rates, mean and 99th-percentile pairwise interference |⟨dᵢ, dⱼ⟩| across SAE decoder directions, activation-correlation distributions across Hungarian-matched features (the **fraction with r > 0.8** is particularly informative because direction preservation does not imply same-context firing), participation ratio PR = (Σσᵢ²)²/Σσᵢ⁴ and effective rank exp(H(pᵢ)) of the activation covariance spectrum, and TwoNN intrinsic dimensionality. The *gap* between linear (PR) and nonlinear (TwoNN) dimensionality is itself diagnostic of curvature changes under quantization.

## Detecting sharpness rigorously

With only 5–7 nominal bit-width data points, sharpness claims must be defended against four threats. **First**, the Schaeffer "mirage" critique: any sharpness reported under one metric must be confirmed under at least two others (the composite Φ takes care of this, but each component is reported separately). **Second**, the discrete-sampling problem: each interpolation strategy above (group-size, mixed-precision, Gaussian-noise, codebook-size) generates a fine grid; if b\* moves smoothly under one knob but the integer curve looks like a step, the apparent step is sampling artifact. **Third**, parametric model selection: BIC contrasts are computed for {constant, linear, sigmoid, step, piecewise-linear, power-law a + c·(1/b)^γ}, with the Kass-Raftery threshold ΔBIC > 10 required to declare "very strong evidence" for sharpness. **Fourth**, bootstrap CIs on the Hill coefficient n must exclude moderate values (≤ 3 in bits⁻¹) for sharpness to be statistically supported.

The most powerful tool is **finite-size scaling**, borrowed from statistical-physics phase-transition analysis and recently applied to grokking (arXiv:2603.24746) and to width-scaling criticality in NNs (Roberts–Yaida–Hanin arXiv:2106.10165; Ringel et al. ICML 2024). Near a true critical point at b_c(∞), the order parameter and susceptibility should obey O(b,d) = d^(-α/ν) · F(d^(1/ν)(b − b_c)), where d is the model width. The Pythia 410M → 6.9B ladder gives four width values varying by ~7×; **the test is whether curves of Φ(b,d) rescaled by d^(-α/ν) versus d^(1/ν)(b − b_c) collapse onto a single master function** when (α, ν, b_c) are jointly fit. Successful collapse — quantified by the Bhattacharjee–Seno residual — is the gold-standard signature of a real thermodynamic-limit transition rather than a finite-size crossover. Failure means the effect is a smooth crossover even if it looks sharp at any single d.

**Susceptibility analysis** computes χ(b) = N·Var_seeds[Φ(b)] across SAE seeds, calibration batches, and layers. A peak at b\* whose height grows with d is independent evidence of criticality, useful when FSS collapse is poorly constrained by limited grid points.

## Baselines and controls that close the most likely loopholes

The experiment becomes credible only because of an aggressive null-baseline structure designed to isolate "loss of superposition" from confounding mechanisms.

**Random-codebook quantization** replaces GPTQ/AWQ-learned codebooks with random codepoints sampled uniformly from [min(W), max(W)] or from N(μ_W, σ_W²). The vertical gap between learned-codebook and random-codebook curves at fixed b quantifies how much of feature recovery depends on the *calibration signal* versus on raw precision. Kuzmin et al. (arXiv:2307.02973) provide the SNR scaffold: INT4 Gaussian-weight quantization achieves ~19.1 dB SNR, equivalent-compression pruning ~5.6 dB; the random-codebook curve should land between these. **At b values where learned and random curves coincide, calibration has lost its grip — a strong information-theoretic signature of the collapse regime.**

**Matched-MSE Gaussian and uniform noise** injection on FP16 weights at variance Δ²/12 isolates the *energetic* part of quantization noise from its *structural* (bounded, deterministic, weight-correlated) part. If matched-MSE Gaussian noise produces the same Φ(b) curve as real quantization, the "critical bit-width" is misnamed — it is a critical noise-power threshold, not a quantization-specific phenomenon. If real quantization produces sharper or earlier collapse than matched-MSE noise, the structure of quantization matters.

**Adversarial random orthogonal rotation** prepends a rotation Q (chosen as the worst of K=1000 random orthogonal samples) to weights before quantization, with Q⁻¹ absorbed downstream. QuIP/QuIP# show favorable rotations help; testing whether b\* shifts under unfavorable rotations isolates whether the transition is intrinsic to representational geometry or a property of the alignment between quantizer grid and feature basis.

**Activation-distribution-shift correction.** Quantization can simply rescale residual-stream activations by per-channel factors α and shift them by β, which looks like feature drift but is trivially repairable. A per-channel affine map fit from quantized to FP16 activations is applied before all comparison metrics; the portion of "collapse" surviving this correction is the real signal. Critically, an FP16-with-injected-mean-shift control measures what fraction of the apparent transition is explained by distribution drift alone.

**Permutation nulls and shuffled-calibration variants** complete the loop. Decoder-column permutation gives chance-level for Hungarian matching; token shuffling gives chance-level for activation correlations; running GPTQ with deliberately mismatched calibration data (code calibration for a chat model) bounds the calibration-noise contribution to apparent transitions.

## Layer-wise, frequency-stratified, and scaling sub-experiments

**Layer-wise dissection** runs three orthogonal scans. *Selective* quantization (only block L at bit-width b, others FP16) maps b\*(L) and tests the HAWQ-V2 prior that middle layers and down_proj/o_proj are most sensitive. *Cumulative* quantization (blocks 0..L at b) measures error accumulation: b\*(L) increasing with L indicates compounding, b\*(L) flat indicates intrinsic per-layer collapse. *Per-component* heatmaps quantize {W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_O, W_up, W_gate, W_down, W_E, W_U} separately at each b, generating a ~9×7 sensitivity matrix per layer.

**Frequency stratification — the cleanest test of the Michaud (arXiv:2303.13506) quantization-model prediction.** SAE features are binned into log-spaced quintiles by firing rate on a 10M-token Pile validation slice (excluding firing rates <10⁻⁵ as dead and >0.1 as polysemantic per Gao et al. 2024 and Chanin & Garriga-Alonso arXiv:2508.16560). For each (b, quintile) cell, preservation fraction is plotted. Three outcomes are possible: **(a) Michaud-consistent** — rare features collapse first, b\*(quintile) tracks log(1/firing_rate), supporting the quantization-model picture (rare quanta have the least representational budget and live in the spectral tail that uniform quantization preferentially truncates, consistent with arXiv:2602.00969); **(b) Uniform collapse** — all quintiles drop at the same b, supporting a global geometric phase transition rather than a feature-by-feature picture; **(c) Inverted ordering** — rare features survive better, mirroring Borobia et al.'s (arXiv:2603.25325) striking Spearman ρ = −1.0 finding under Wanda pruning. Outcome (c) would be the most surprising and would directly contradict the naive Michaud prediction for the quantization channel, indicating that pruning and quantization act on different parts of the feature spectrum.

**Pruning at matched compression** runs Wanda (arXiv:2306.11695), SparseGPT (arXiv:2301.00774), and magnitude pruning at sparsities s = 1 − b/16 ∈ {0.5, 0.625, 0.75, 0.8125, 0.875}, plotted against bits-per-weight on a common axis. The qualitative question — does the SAE-recovery curve have the same shape under quantization and pruning at matched compression? — has not been answered in the literature for LLMs. Borobia's pruning finding plus the spectral-flattening prediction for quantization make a divergent outcome the most likely.

**Model-size scaling** within Pythia (410M, 1.4B, 2.8B, 6.9B) and Gemma-2 (2B, 9B) supplies the d-axis for finite-size scaling. SAE-to-model-width ratio is held constant at 32× to prevent trivial confounding. Three scaling hypotheses are pre-registered: b\* constant in bits (per-feature precision sets the floor), b\* ~ a + β·log(d) (each near-orthogonal direction in d-dimensional space needs O(log d) bits to address), or b\* saturating/decreasing (wider models have redundancy that absorbs precision loss). Successful FSS collapse selects among them.

## Pre-registered success criteria and what we learn either way

A pre-registration is filed before any quantization runs: the hypothesis is *supported* if at least three of the four following conditions hold on Gemma-2 2B and replicate qualitatively on Gemma-2 9B and at least two Pythia sizes. **(1)** The Hill coefficient n for Φ(b) has a 95% bootstrap CI excluding n<3 across HQQ, GPTQ, and AQLM. **(2)** A step or high-n sigmoid model dominates the BIC ranking over linear/power-law alternatives with ΔBIC > 10. **(3)** Susceptibility χ(b) peaks at b\* and the peak height grows monotonically with model width d. **(4)** Finite-size scaling collapse achieves a Bhattacharjee–Seno residual below 0.1 with consistent (b_c, ν, α) estimates across both order parameters P1 and P2.

The hypothesis is *refuted* if Φ(b) follows a smooth power-law or low-n logistic with no susceptibility peak, if b\* drifts by >1 bit across quantization methods, if matched-MSE Gaussian noise reproduces the entire transition (reducing the claim to "critical noise variance, not critical bit-width"), or if affine activation correction restores >80% of the apparent collapse.

The Michaud-style frequency stratification yields a separate result regardless of outcome (1–4): if rare features collapse first under quantization while surviving under pruning at matched compression, that establishes a direct asymmetry between the two compression channels with mechanistic implications (quantization noise is additive in activation magnitude; pruning noise is multiplicative in weight magnitude). This finding alone would be publishable independent of the phase-transition result.

## Conclusion

The protocol's distinctive contribution is methodological: it brings statistical-physics-grade phase-transition machinery — finite-size scaling, susceptibility analysis, BIC-based parametric model comparison, multiple continuous-bit-width interpolation strategies, and an aggressive null-baseline structure — to a question that the existing quantization-interpretability literature has so far only described qualitatively. The strongest single piece of leverage is the Pythia scaling ladder combined with the four-way bit-width interpolation: together they convert a 5-point integer grid into a two-dimensional continuous surface where data collapse can either confirm a true thermodynamic-limit transition at b\* ≈ 2.5–3 bits (the value implied by Liu et al. 2026 and consistent with the SNR floor at which GPTQ calibration loses its grip) or expose the apparent cliff as a smooth crossover dressed up by discrete sampling. Either outcome materially advances the field — and the Michaud-vs-Borobia rare/common feature-ordering question is resolvable as a side effect of the same data collection. The experiment is feasible on the stated hardware within roughly five wall-clock days of compute, and the resulting figure — feature recovery versus effective bits-per-weight, with random-codebook and matched-MSE baselines bracketing the learned-quantization curve, replicated across model widths with susceptibility insets — would be a citable benchmark independent of which direction the hypothesis breaks.
